How to read the chart

The centre is the market (Nifty 50). A sector to the right is stronger than the market; above the middle it's accelerating. Sectors normally drift clockwise through these four stages:

Improving (top-left)

Still weaker than the market, but momentum has turned up. These are the potential next leaders.

→ Add to watchlist, early nibble

Leading (top-right)

Stronger than the market and still gaining. The current outperformers doing the heavy lifting.

→ Ride the trend, add on dips

Weakening (bottom-right)

Still strong, but momentum is cooling. Leaders that may be getting tired.

→ Book some profit, tighten stops

Lagging (bottom-left)

Weaker than the market and still sliding. Best left alone until they start to improve.

→ Avoid / stay out

How to use it (5 steps)

  1. Pick a view: Weekly for the big-picture trend, Daily for a faster, noisier read.
  2. Look at the tails. A long, straight tail = a persistent move. A tail that hooks back means the sector is losing conviction.
  3. Favour sectors in Leading and rotating up into it from Improving. Be cautious on names sliding from Weakening toward Lagging.
  4. Hit ▶ Play or drag the slider to watch how sectors rotated over the last few months — direction matters more than the single dot.
  5. Confirm with the actual stocks before acting. RRG shows the tide, not the entry. It's a map, not a trade signal.

Frequently asked questions

What is a Relative Rotation Graph (RRG)?

RRG is a chart, created by Julius de Kempenaer, that shows how a group of things (here, sectors) are performing relative to a benchmark (the Nifty 50) — and whether that relative performance is getting better or worse. Instead of one line per sector, it plots each sector as a single moving dot in a two-dimensional space, so you can compare all sectors at a glance and see which way the money is rotating.

What do the two axes mean?

The horizontal axis is RS-Ratio — how strong a sector is versus the Nifty. 100 means it's moving exactly in line with the market; above 100 it is outperforming, below 100 it is underperforming. The vertical axis is RS-Momentum — whether that strength is speeding up or slowing down. Above 100 the sector is gaining ground; below 100 it is losing ground. The exact centre (100, 100) is the market itself.

What do the four quadrants mean?

Leading (top-right): stronger than the market and still improving — the current winners. Weakening (bottom-right): still stronger than the market but momentum is fading — winners getting tired. Lagging (bottom-left): weaker than the market and still falling — the laggards. Improving (top-left): still weaker than the market but momentum has turned up — possible future leaders. Healthy markets rotate clockwise: Improving → Leading → Weakening → Lagging → Improving.

How do I read the tails?

Each dot drags a tail of its recent path. A long, straight tail means a steady, persistent move — strong conviction. A tail that is short or hooking back on itself means the sector is losing conviction and may be about to change direction. The direction the tail points matters more than where the single dot sits today.

Why is every sector bunched near the middle?

Sectors are large, diversified baskets, so they rarely move very far from the overall market — that is normal. What you are looking for is not distance from the centre but which quadrant a sector is in and which way its tail is heading. Increase the trail length or switch to the Daily view to see the rotation more clearly.

Daily or Weekly — which should I use?

Weekly smooths out day-to-day noise and is best for swing and positional decisions — most people should anchor here. Daily reacts faster and is useful for timing, but it is noisier and easy to over-read. A good habit: form your view on Weekly, then use Daily for fine-tuning.

Can I buy a sector the moment it enters Leading?

Not on its own. RRG shows the structural tide, not a precise entry. A single hop across a line is often just noise. Look for a sector that has been travelling in a consistent direction for several periods, then drill into the actual leading stocks within that sector to find an entry. Treat RRG as a map, not a trade signal.

How is each sector index built?

Each sector here is a cap-weighted basket of its real constituent stocks (for example, the Bank sector is HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank, SBI, and so on), weighted by market value just like the official NSE sectoral indices. This is computed from this project's own price database, so it needs no external data feed.

What does "stocks with good momentum" mean?

Click any sector row to reveal the individual stocks inside it that are in a clear uptrend. A stock qualifies on a classic trend filter: its 50-day moving average is above its 200-day moving average (the longer-term trend is up) and the current price is above the 50-day average (it is trading above its own trend). Each stock chip also shows how far price is above its 50-DMA. This is an absolute price-trend check on the stock itself — separate from where its sector sits on the RRG, which is measured relative to the Nifty.

What are "sub-sectors" and how do I see them?

Broad sectors can hide what's happening underneath. "Financials", for instance, splits into NBFCs, Insurance, Capital Markets and Housing Finance, which often rotate very differently. Use the Show dropdown to pick a single group (e.g. Healthcare → Pharma, Hospitals, Diagnostics, CDMO/API), or switch the toggle to "Incl. sub-sectors" to overlay every finer index at once. Sub-sectors are marked SUB in the table. In all there are 45 indices — 17 broad sectors and 28 sub-sectors.

What is the benchmark and why the Nifty 50?

The benchmark is the Nifty 50 (via the NIFTYBEES ETF) — India's broad large-cap market. "Sector rotation" is about money moving between sectors within the market, so measuring each sector against the whole market is the natural reference point.

How fresh is the data, and how often does it update?

Values are end-of-session snapshots from this project's daily price database — not live tick-by-tick. The "updated" date in the header shows the latest session included. Re-running the tool after the database refreshes brings it fully current.

Is this a buy/sell recommendation?

No. This is an educational and research tool to understand market structure and sector trends. It does not account for your goals, risk, or position sizing, and is not investment advice. Always do your own research or consult a registered advisor before investing.
